How We Work

Engineering is a lot of work. Sometimes science can make it easier. Or can make what you already do work better. Many companies, however, don't have the skills in house to bring advanced computer science to bear.

We are a group of computer scientists with many years of industry experience. We operate on the theory that helping you help yourself is usually more cost-efficient than hiring someone to do the work for you. We come in as much as you need. It could be a single day of discussion, brainstorming, or training. Or it might be a repeating engagement, as needed. Our job is to help you do your job without us.

Contact us today. An initial discussion and problem assessment are free: no obligation, no fee.

Statistics and Big Data

Dublin/Pleasonton, CA, USA. Early March 2015, TBA. 6-9 p.m. $199.

A fun evening of statistics! You'll learn about how to ask the right questions to understand your data. The hard part of big data is not the data analysis, but finding the right questions to ask. Making sure those questions are statistically meaningful turns out to be important. This session can help. And you'll go home with code to help you keep going the next day.

  • Data and its different flavors (categorical, ordinal, etc.)
  • Experimental design
  • Probability. Independence. Conditional probability. Bayes Theorem.
  • Distributions. Normal distributions and the CLT. Significance and p-values.
  • Estimation, confidence, and hypothesis testing.
  • Frequentists vs Bayesians. Counting vs probability.
  • Inference, t-distributions, comparing two means, anova, chi-square.
  • Regression, least squares, outliers; multiple regression.
  • Examples and applications!

Statistics and Big Data

This evening course, rather than focusing purely on the software tools of big data, helps you learn to ask the right questions. Just as important, the course helps you learn how to phrase questions and understand the results. All of this, of course, in the familiar context of common python tools. The course provides practical examples and experience with Scikit Learn and other relevant python libraries useful for analyzing big data.

The first day introduces you to fundamental statistical methods before applying those methods in a big data context. We begin with basic concepts of data, categorization, and experimental design. The course then discusses the foundations of probability theory with an emphasis on the geometric and intuitive nature of probability rather than on the purely mathematical: independence and conditional probability, Bayes theorem and Bayesian inference, significance, and useful distributions. Finally, the course discusses least squares, regression, and other techniques for distinguishing data sets.

Throughout the presentation, you will explore the theory using simple constructs in python. We'll work practical examples on real problems (though with data sets that let us work quickly).

Prerequisites: You should be mathematically literate, but no specific mathematical background is required. You should have at least some experience with programming and a basic knowledge of python.

Recommendation Systems

This evening course provides an introduction to recommendation engines in the context of familiar python tools. It moreover provides an introduction to the useful domain-specific python tools scikit-learn, statsmodels, and pandas. The goal of the course is to help you learn to think about recommendation, to evaluate different approaches, and to understand the advantages and pitfalls of different problem approaches. Using familiar software tools, we explore hands-on solutions to sample problems in order to provide a straight-forward bridge to real-world challenges.

In Recommendation Engines with Python we will discuss the two broad categories of recommendation engines, collaborative filtering and content-based recommendation. Useful as these techniques are, however, without an understanding of some of the basic machine learning techniques that accompany them, a properly functioning recommendation engine is unlikely to result. We will therefore discuss clustering techniques, dimensionality reduction, and related data structures and techniques, such as locality sensitive hashing, that help us actually find the data we desire.

We will also talk about scalability and what factors influence design in the face of today's data sets that are (or that one expects to become) impractical to analyze on a single machine.

Equally important, the course will discuss when \textit{not} to use a recommendation engine.

Prerequisites: You should be mathematically literate, but no specific mathematical background is required. You should have at least some experience with programming and a basic knowledge of python.
